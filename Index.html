Section 01

The Core Feeling Hooks

These are scroll-stopping opening lines — the ones that make the right person pause and think "that's me." Lead with one of these on every post, every email, every ad. No explanation, no label, no diagnosis. Just the feeling — spoken back to them clearly.

```
"Tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix."

This is the most universally recognised description of the state. Clinical research confirms it's the defining complaint. Use as an opening line alone — nothing else on the graphic.

"You keep functioning. But you don't know for how much longer."

Speaks to the quietly unravelling professional. Acknowledges the performance without praising it. Creates a mirror moment.

"You used to care about things. Now you just manage them."

Captures the shift from engagement to maintenance mode — one of the most painful aspects of the state. Instantly recognisable.

"Something has gone quiet inside you."

Speaks to the spiritual displacement profile. Gentle, poetic, non-clinical. Ideal for Instagram and slower content.

"You've tried the things. They helped for a while. Then you were back here."

Acknowledges the failed recovery cycles without blame. Creates immediate trust — you're not being sold another quick fix.

"The version of yourself you used to be doesn't feel that far away. Just — unreachable."

The most emotionally resonant hook for those mid-recovery. The gap between who they were and who they are now is the lived experience of this state.

"You give a lot. To everyone. And you don't know who gives back to you."

Speaks directly to the compassion-fatigued giver profile — carers, parents, healthcare workers, teachers. High-share potential.

"You're not broken. You're running on the wrong signal."

The reframe hook — validates their experience while offering a new frame. Leads naturally into The Beacon Telegraph's mission language. Strong for bios and profile descriptions.

"When did resting start feeling like something you have to earn?"

A question rather than a statement — creates a pause, invites reflection. Excellent for carousels and email subjects. The guilt-about-rest pain point is near-universal.

"There's a part of you that remembers what it felt like to be fully yourself. It hasn't gone anywhere."

The positive mirror — meets people in the loss but immediately pivots to hope. Perfect as a closing slide on a carousel or a sign-off on an email.

"You're excellent at keeping things together. That's not the same as being okay."

Speaks to the high achiever and the quietly unravelling professional. Acknowledges the competence while naming the gap.

"What if the exhaustion isn't the problem — it's the message?"

Reframes fatigue as intelligent communication. Opens into the nervous-system-as-signal language of the Protocol. Strong for mid-funnel content.

```
Section 02

Language to Avoid — and What to Say Instead

The words we choose either open the door or close it. These swaps are based on how people describe their own experience versus the clinical or marketing language that distances them from it.

Avoid Use Instead Why
Burnout "Running on empty" / "That particular kind of tired" / "When everything has cost more than it gave back" People in this state often don't self-identify as "burnt out" — the word feels like a failure label. The feeling is immediate and unambiguous.
Recovery / healing Restoration / return / coming back to yourself "Recovery" implies sickness. "Return" implies you were always whole — you just drifted from yourself. Completely different emotional register.
Self-care Practice / restoration / the work of return "Self-care" has been so heavily commodified it has become meaningless — and often triggers eye-rolling in the high-achiever audience. "Practice" carries depth.
Wellness / wellbeing Steadiness / frequency / signal / inner life "Wellness" reads as an industry. The Beacon Telegraph language is philosophical and maritime — it belongs to no trend.
Mental health The quality of your inner life / what's happening underneath Not because mental health is not important — but because leading with it triggers clinical associations and may repel people who don't identify as struggling with mental health.
Stress management Working with the nervous system / the body as signal "Stress management" implies control and performance. The Beacon Telegraph framework is about listening and responding — fundamentally different posture.
Fix / solve / overcome Return to / restore / find your way back to You don't fix a person. The Protocol's premise is that what they need is already in them — it just needs to be returned to. The language must match the philosophy.
Programme / course / system Practice / protocol / sequence "Programme" triggers images of rigid curricula and pressure to complete. "Practice" and "protocol" suggest something you return to — consistent with the Protocol's approach.
Transform / transformation Restoration / return / a different quality of daily life "Transformation" has been overused to the point of distrust, and implies the current self is inadequate. The Beacon Telegraph's premise is the opposite.
